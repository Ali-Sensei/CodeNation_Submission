Legal Impact on Digital Projects

In the digital age, the internet has allowed us to do various tasks in which we send out our personal information without really considering the consequences. When we use social media such as Facebook and Twitter, we upload personal information, such as contact information and images, and we do freely.

But then in May 2018, GDPR was introduced, a measure of European privacy that changes the way businesses collect, store, and use information of customers. It is an assurance that customer information is secure and protected.

What that means for a business is now it is important to remember the rights of the citizens, namely:

The Right to Access

People have the rights to request any personal information that is held by a business as well as information on how this information is being used. The business must provide this information free of charge. Digital projects that involve personal data must have this data easily accessible.

The Right to Erasure

People have the rights to have their information removed from a business when they stop being a customer of that business, or they do not give their consent anymore, and businesses must comply with this. For a digital project, a system needs to be in place where deletion of personal data is accomplishable.

The Right of Transfer

People have the rights to transfer their information from one business to another in a portable format. Businesses must use a common form of data storage that other businesses can read. By using commonly used formats, digital projects can comply with this and have the personal data portable if need be.

The Right to be Informed

People have the rights to be informed when their personal information is being gathered by a business, and also the fact that people must opt in for their information to be gathered explicitly, which wasn’t the case before GDPR. A digital project should have some sort of notice which includes a method where a user can give explicit consent to storage of personal data and explicitly opt in for services.

The Right to Rectification

People have the rights to update their data when it is incomplete or incorrect, and businesses have an obligation to update this information. Digital projects need methods where personal data can be easily modified to rectify incorrect information.

The Right to Object

When people desire, they have the right to prevent any processing of their data for marketing directed towards them, and businesses must immediately stop upon receiving the request. A system needs to be in place for a digital project to stop using personal data in marketing such as removing a person from a mailing list.

The Right to be Notified

In situations where a person’s information has been compromised, such as from a data breach, the business has the obligation to notify the person within 72 hours. Since the digital project may include contact information of the person, an email/letter could be sent to the person or a call may be made.

The Right to Restrict Processing

Similar to the right to object, in this situation, the person can request for their information not to be used in any processing by the business, i.e. the business may store the information, but not actually use it. Digital projects need to have a system in place where it is possible to store the person’s information while not being used at all.

Overall

Considering the rights that the GDPR gives to the individual, it is important for a business to meet the criteria to accept GDPR. Since in the realm of the digital age, customer information is stored using computer systems, and is possibly actively used by computer systems such as an automated mailing list system, it is important for a business to follow the rules of the GDPR otherwise heavy penalties could follow; up to 4% of business annual revenue or 20 million Euros.
